Introduction
Bitmarkets is a free and open source protocol and example client for a decentralized marketplace which uses Bitcoin as it's currency and Bitmessage as it's communications transport protocol. Mutual security deposits between buyer and seller on individual transactions are used ensure incentives are aligned for completing the transaction without the need for either reputation systems or trusted 3rd parties.

This document is a non-technical overview of how the Bitmarkets client and protocol work. For a more technical details, see the Bitmarkets protocol specification.
Motivation
The current state of commerce is one of centralized marketplaces and payment services that extract high fees, impose and abuse excessive control and remove any hope of privacy from users. As more commerce moves online many consumers may find their lifetime history of purchases (including books, personal items and location details) for sale to advertisers, employers, stalkers, curious neighbors, political opponents and government agencies.

An ideal system would be one of secure private transactions directly between producers and consumers without middle men collecting data or adding fees. Such systems are now feasible by combining recently developed technologies for anonymous decentralized payments and anonymous decentralized messaging.

Implementation
Client
To use the marketplace, a client application which implements the Bitmarkets protocol is needed. The client is used to post, browse and execute transactions and contains a bitcoin wallet which is used to construct the multi-signature two-party escrow transactions used for security deposits, payments and refunds.
Identities
The client uses the Bitmessage anonymous decentralized messaging network and Tor (a source location hiding communications network) to broadcast sales and exchange transaction related messages. Upon starting the client, a Bitmessage identity (a public encryption key) is generated which, like an email address, will be used for communications on the network.
Depositing Funds
In order to complete a transaction, both buyer and seller must have sufficient funds in their wallets for escrow and payment. The client contains a Bitcoin wallet in which these funds can be deposited and withdrawn.
Posting Sales
Using the client, a seller specifies the region, category, title, description and price and broadcasts this to a specific Bitmessage channel. These posts are then relayed throughout the Bitmessage network where other clients see them and make them browsable by buyers.
Requesting a Purchase
Using the client, buyers can browse regions and categories to find products/services for sale. If/when they choose an item to buy, the client sends a Bitmessage to the requesting a purchase.
Accepting/Rejecting a Purchase
If the seller accepts a purchase request, an accept message is sent to the buyer and the client automatically sends reject messages to any other purchase requesters.

Escrow System
Problems with 3rd Party Escrow
Choice of Escrow Ratios
The purpose of escrow is to ensure both parties have sufficiently strong incentives to complete transactions. The approach of this analysis is to look at critical limits in order to extrapolate a conservative range of escrow ratios that ensure a functioning market.
Simplifying Assumptions
It's assumed a functioning market is one where consistently malicious actors will on average go bankrupt assuming their bankroll is small compared to the volume of the market. It's also assumed this is an efficient market where margins are low so seller profit margins are small enough to ignore (i.e. for our conservative escrow estimates to cover).
No Deposits
With no deposits, an anonymous buyer has no direct economic incentive to complete the payment on delivery of the purchased item and even a single malicious buyer could bring down the entire market by ordering all items and not paying for any of them. Likewise, a single malicious seller might burry the market with fake sales which are never delivered. So our deposit must be greater than zero for a market to reliably function in the absence of a reputation system or trusted 3rd party escrow.
High Deposits
The larger the ratio of mutual security deposit to payment, the greater incentive each side has to faithfully complete the transaction until the value becomes so large that one or both sides find in too costly to withhold escrow release to punish the other side for not completing the transaction and may accept being defrauded in order to minimize their losses.

The picture so far is that possible losses on both side should be above zero but not above the amount either party is willing to loose in order to satisfy punitive motivations. Terms
  • X - price of item (the good or service)
  • R - escrow to item price ratio
There is a spectrum of fraud situations but for simplicity (and because the client only offers full refunds or payments as options) only the following two will be considered for now and discuss the issue of the quality of the item delivered later.
Seller Fraud - Fraudulent Payment Request
The seller fails to deliver item and requests payment.

Buyer stands to loose: (1+R)*X (their escrow plus their payment)
Seller stands to loose: R*X (their escrow)
Buyer Fraud - Fraudulent Refund Request
The seller delivers acceptable item but the buyer requests a refund.

Buyer stands to loose: R*X (their escrow)
Seller stands to loose: (1+R)*X

For the first version of the Bitmarkets client we choose to use a 1-1 ratio as item cost to per-party security deposit. That is, for a item costing X, the escrow transaction locks 2X (1X for deposit and 1X for payment) from the buyer and 1X from the seller - a total of 3X.
